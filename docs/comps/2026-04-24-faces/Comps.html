§02  About

The short version, in first person.

Based Remote. Mountain West, USA.
License Registered Architect, USA.
Years in 15+ across field, design, and sponsorship.
Prior VP, development at a mid-market sponsor. Before that: design-side and field-side at a national GC.

I'm Conor Keilty. CBKDI is my consulting practice. I spent fifteen-plus years on the sponsor side of real-estate development — field construction first, then architecture, then deal underwriting and pre-development — before I realized that most of the problems operators actually hire me for are not deal problems. They are systems problems.

The spreadsheet says one thing. The field says another. The partner deck says a third. Somebody has to sit in the middle and decide what is real, and then put the decision into a shape the next person can use. That is the work.

I take on a small number of retainers and fixed-scope engagements a year. Recent work has been a capital-stack model that stress-tests on a slider, a QuickBooks-to-CCTD automation for a commercial GC, and an investor-facing scenario explorer for a community-development project in Colorado.

If that sounds like the shape of your problem, the contact form is below.

The spreadsheet is not the project.

Every sponsor I have worked with has a moment — usually around month four — when the model and the field stop agreeing. The job, from then on, is to figure out which one is lying.

Essay · 2026-03-14 · 8 min read · Rev 2026.04

The first sponsor I worked for had a spreadsheet that was, I am not exaggerating, the company. Every deal lived in it. Every draw, every assumption, every decision that anyone had ever walked into a partner meeting and defended. When the founder left the office, the file came with him on a thumb drive. When the office flooded, the thumb drive came out of his pocket dry.

I inherited that spreadsheet the week I was promoted into pre-development. Within ninety days it had diverged from the field in ways I did not yet have the vocabulary for. A steel package we had modeled at one number had come back at a different one. A zoning variance we had assumed would land in two months had landed in six. The model, beautifully consistent internally, no longer described a project that existed anywhere outside of it.

§02What the field knows first

Here is the thing that took me a decade to name. The field knows before the model does. The superintendent who has been running this trade for twenty years can feel a crew slowing down in week two; the model will not notice until the draw request comes in late in month three. By the time the spreadsheet can tell you the project is in trouble, the project has been in trouble for sixty days.

A model is a snapshot of what you were willing to believe the day you built it. The project is whatever is actually happening. These are not the same thing and they were never going to be.

This is not a failure of modeling. It is a description of what models are. The spreadsheet's job is to hold a decision long enough for a room of people to agree on it. Once the decision is made, the model's usefulness starts decaying on a clock that nobody in the room is watching. ¹

FN 01 The "clock nobody is watching" framing is borrowed from a conversation with a former construction partner who put it better than I ever will: "the model is fresh for about as long as a gallon of milk."
